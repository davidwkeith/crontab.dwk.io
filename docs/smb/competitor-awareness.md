# Competitor Awareness

Understanding what other local businesses in the same space are doing online. Reference for the webmaster agent — applied during `/anglesite:design-interview` and quarterly reviews. Not user-facing documentation.

This is not competitive analysis in the corporate sense. It's about helping the owner understand what customers in their market expect from a website, and where the owner can stand out.

## Why it matters

Customers compare. When someone searches for "plumber in [town]," they see 3–5 businesses and pick one. Their decision is based on what they find — or can't find — on each website. If every competitor has online booking and the owner doesn't, that's a disadvantage. If every competitor has a generic template site and the owner has a genuine story with real photos, that's an advantage.

The goal is awareness, not obsession. Check what competitors are doing, learn from it, and move on.

## During `/anglesite:design-interview`

Ask:

1. **"Who are the other [business type] businesses in your area?"** — The owner usually knows their 3–5 main competitors by name.
2. **"Do they have websites? Are any of them good?"** — The owner's gut reaction is useful. "Their site is way better than mine" reveals expectations. "None of them have a real website" reveals an opportunity.
3. **"What do they do well that you'd like to do too?"** — Not copying, but learning. Online booking, good photos, clear pricing, active blog.
4. **"What do you do differently or better?"** — This becomes the website's differentiator. Personal service, family-owned, specialized expertise, better quality, unique products.

## What to look for

When the owner names competitors, spend a few minutes checking their websites. Note:

### Content and features
- Do they have online booking, ordering, or scheduling?
- Do they show prices or require a call for a quote?
- Do they have a blog or post updates?
- Do they have customer reviews or testimonials?
- Do they have clear service areas and hours?
- Do they have good photos (real vs. stock)?

### Basics
- Is the site mobile-friendly?
- Is it fast or slow?
- Is the contact info easy to find?
- Does the Google Business Profile have reviews, photos, and current hours?

### Gaps the owner can fill
- If competitors use stock photos, real photos are a differentiator
- If competitors don't blog, regular content gives a search advantage
- If competitors don't show prices, transparency builds trust
- If competitors don't have testimonials, social proof stands out
- If competitors have slow, cluttered sites, a clean fast site feels professional

## What to do with the findings

### Raise the floor

If competitors offer something that customers now expect (online booking for salons, menus for restaurants, portfolios for photographers), the owner needs it too. This isn't about copying — it's about meeting baseline expectations.

### Find the gap

Every market has something nobody is doing well. Common gaps:

- **Nobody shows prices.** The first business to show transparent pricing wins the "I don't want to call for a quote" customer segment.
- **Nobody blogs.** The first business to write useful content about common customer questions will rank in search results that all competitors are missing.
- **Nobody has real photos.** The first business with genuine photos of their work, team, and space builds more trust than all the stock photos combined.
- **Nobody is accessible.** An accessible website that works on every device is an advantage — not just ethically, but practically. Competitors with inaccessible sites are losing customers.

### Play to strengths

Help the owner articulate what makes them different and build the website around it:

- **Experience** — "30 years in business" is a trust signal. Feature it prominently.
- **Specialization** — "We only do residential electrical" is more compelling than "we do everything."
- **Story** — "Third-generation family farm" or "I started this business in my garage" — people connect with stories. Competitors with generic corporate sites can't compete with authenticity.
- **Values** — Sustainability, community involvement, fair pricing, accessibility. If the owner cares about something, the website should show it.

## Quarterly check

During the quarterly review (aligned with `/anglesite:update`):

- "Have any new competitors opened?" — New businesses may change the landscape.
- "Have any competitors updated their websites?" — If a competitor just added online booking, the owner should know.
- "Are customers mentioning competitors?" — "I almost went to [competitor] but..." reveals what customers compare.

Don't make this a big production. A 5-minute scan of competitor sites once a quarter is enough. The owner should spend 95% of their time on their own business, not watching competitors.

## What NOT to do

- **Don't copy competitor content.** Duplicate content hurts search rankings and is ethically wrong.
- **Don't mention competitors on the website.** No "why we're better than [competitor]" pages. It looks insecure and gives the competitor free publicity.
- **Don't price-match based on websites.** Competitor pricing on their website may be outdated, loss-leader, or for a different service level. Compete on value, not just price.
- **Don't over-invest in competitor monitoring.** Tools like SEMrush or Ahrefs are expensive and overkill for a small local business. A quarterly manual check is sufficient.
